## Project Overview

This project aims to answer the following three questions using the dataset:

1. How does the volume of green coffee production in a country correlate with its Human Development Index (HDI) score?
2. Are there specific regions where the Resource Paradox is most pronounced, showing high production but low development?
3. How has the relationship between coffee production and human development evolved over the recent years (2020 to 2023)?

The global coffee industry presents a striking example of the resource paradox: countries that serve as the primary engines of production often experience lower levels of human development compared to the nations that consume their products. This project investigates the correlation between a nation's status as a top coffee producer and its corresponding human development metrics, examining how agricultural export output translates — or fails to translate — into improved standards of living for the populations involved.

The background for these questions is rooted in **resource curse theory**, which suggests that countries with an abundance of natural resources often face stagnant economic growth and poor development outcomes. While coffee is a renewable agricultural commodity, reports highlight that many small-scale farmers remain in poverty despite their essential role in the global supply chain. Understanding this paradox is critical because it highlights systemic inequalities in global trade and the ethical implications of consumption.

## Datasets

To investigate these relationships, we use two publicly available datasets:

**Dataset 1 — FAOSTAT (Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations)**  
[https://www.fao.org/faostat](https://www.fao.org/faostat)  
This dataset provides coffee production data maintained by the FAO, an agency responsible for standardizing agricultural data reported by national governments worldwide. It includes 409 rows of annual green coffee production figures (in metric tonnes) recorded between 2020 and 2024. Its global scope makes it a reliable source for academic research, though some rows contain missing production values and sovereign territories without country status are excluded.

**Dataset 2 — Human Development Report (United Nations Development Programme)**  
[https://hdr.undp.org/data-center](https://hdr.undp.org/data-center)  
This dataset is produced by the UNDP's Human Development Report Office and draws on data validated from international statistical bodies and national governments. It contains longitudinal Human Development Index (HDI) scores for over 190 countries, with this project focusing on scores from 2020 to 2023. Each record includes a composite HDI score derived from life expectancy, schooling, and gross national income. A key limitation is that reducing complex human conditions to a single composite score can mask systemic unfairness and internal inequality.

By combining these datasets, we are able to analyze patterns across economic (coffee production volume) and developmental (HDI) variables and trace how these relationships have shifted over time across different world regions.

---

## Why This Matters

Understanding the relationship between resource production and human development is important because it reveals structural inequalities embedded in global trade systems. Coffee is one of the most valuable commodities in the world, yet many of the countries responsible for its production rank among the lowest on human development measures. This disconnect has real consequences: stagnating wages, limited access to education and healthcare, and sustained poverty among farming communities.

Regional analysis further reveals that geography plays a critical role in how the paradox manifests. Producers in Latin America generally achieve higher development levels than those in Africa or the Asia-Pacific region for comparable production levels, pointing to the influence of national policy, trade agreements, and historical economic structures. Future research should incorporate localized wage data and environmental impact metrics to better capture on-the-ground realities for individual farmers.

---

## Ethical Considerations and Limitations

There are important ethical limitations to consider when interpreting this analysis. The HDI relies on national averages, which can mask internal regional disparities and the actual conditions of individual farmers. The data lacks the granularity to address localized issues such as wage inequality or labor rights violations within producing countries. As a result, findings should be interpreted as broad associations within these datasets rather than definitive causal conclusions about the lived experiences of all people within a given nation.
